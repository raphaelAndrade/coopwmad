Cover Letter

What is a cover letter?

A cover letter is a document sent with your resume to provide additional information on your skills and experience. It’s a knock on the manager’s door, where you have only 2 minutes to introduce yourself (elevator speech).

The letter provides detailed information on why you are qualified for the job you are applying for. Don’t simply repeat what’s on your resume -- rather, include specific information on why you’re a strong match for the employer’s job requirements. Think of your cover letter as a sales pitch that will market your credentials and help you get the interview. As such, you want to make sure your cover letter makes the best impression on the person who is reviewing it.

Salutation

If you know the name of the hiring manager, great!

If you don’t know their name, here are some simple and appropriate options:

  • Dear [Insert Name],
  • Dear Hiring Manager,
  • Dear [Company Name] Team,

Never use these salutations in a cover letter:

  • Hi,
  • Hello,
  • Dear Sir/Madam,
  • To Whom it May Concern,

Intro Paragraph

Always create a positive and personable introduction!

  • Mention the job title and the company
  • Show enthusiasm for the job
  • Briefly state your most related skills or achievement

Never use these in your opening paragraph:

  • I’m [insert name]
  • How are you?
  • I am a co-op student
An example of a BAD opening paragraph:

Hi,

I’m Raphael. I’m a co-op student at Cornerstone. I’m interested in the job of Web Development. I am studying Web Development and I have also worked in mobile development in my own country so I know about it. I worked for my company for 3 years.

An example of a GOOD opening paragraph:

Dear Hiring Manager,

I was excited to see your job listing for the Lead Front-end position at Jelly Design Agency. As a dynamic Front-end Developer with over two years of professional experience executing web development, working with Design Team and Project management. I’m confident that I would be a valuable asset to the team at Jelly Design.

Body Paragraphs

Explain how you can help the company achieve its goals, showcasing your relevant experience, education, and skills.

  • Highlights of your background
  • Why are you a good fit for the company?
  • Why is the company a good fit for you?
  • Show your knowledge of the company
  • Look at the job description – what are they looking for in a candidate?
  • Look at your skills – how do they match the job description?
  • Evidence of 2 – 3 related examples to job description.
An example of a GOOD body paragraph:
In my current role as a Junior Web Developer at 123 Technologies, I design, code, and implement websites for a variety of clients. In my latest project, I redesigned a website for a privacy software company, which helped increase their sales by 25% this quarter. Through working collaboratively with marketing, design, and product teams, I have developed strong communication and teamwork skills. I can bring my technical skills and passion for collaboration to the XYZ Company team.

Call-to-action Paragraph

End your cover letter with a strong statement that is proactive and invites the hiring manager to take the next step, like call you for an interview.

An example of a GOOD body paragraph:
I look forward to discussing the Lead Front-end position and my qualifications with you in more detail. I’m available to talk at your convenience. I’ll be in touch next week to follow up and to make sure you’ve received my application.

Sign-off

Use a professional closing and sign your name.

  • Sincerely,
  • Regards,
  • Best Regards,
Sincerely,
Raphael Andrade

References: available on request

You do not need to include details about your references on your resume. However, if requested, you should have at least 2 professional references to provide to a new employer. You can ask your Instructor or Co-op Advisor to be a reference for you. Usually, an employer will ask for references after an interview. It is often a good sign that you were successful at the interview stage!

hobbies content

You do not need to include hobbies or interests on your resume. However, if you think that your interests are related to the job, then it could be a good idea to include them.

If you are applying for a company that sells outdoor clothing and equipment, and you enjoy climbing and running marathons… write that!

If you are applying for a travel agency position and you have travelled to 20 countries… write that!
