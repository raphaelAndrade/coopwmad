About Interviews

Interviews are designed to find out these three items:

  • 1) Can you do the job? (experience and skills)
  • 2) Will you do the job? (motivation)
  • 3) Do you fit in? (culture and team)

Research the company

  • Website, LinkedIn, social media accounts
    • Company culture/values
    • Products/services
    • Company size
    • Hot topics in the industry
  • Customer reviews (Google, Facebook, etc).
  • Employee reviews (Glassdoor, Indeed)
    • Salary reviews
    • Interview reviews

Review the job posting … again

  • Why are you a good fit?
  • What areas are your strengths?
  • What examples from past projects or experience are most relevant to this job?
  • What areas do you need to study more?
  • Make sure you understand what the job will entail.

Prepare your questions

  • ALWAYS prepare a list of questions to ask the interviewer(s)
    • Not doing so makes you appear unprepared or uninterested
  • About the job itself (logistics, team size, etc)
  • About their performance expectations/KPIs
  • About the company and its goals (details not found on website)
  • Anything else that you’re interested in!
  • Examples of questions to ask:
    • What would an average day be like?
    • How would my work be monitored and how often would I be appraised?
    • What career paths have other graduates followed in this company? Will I have any opportunity to use my foreign language skills?
    • Will I be working in a team?
    • What is the makeup of these teams?
    • What is the makeup of these teams?
    • What are the company’s development plans and targets over the next five years?

Research common questions

There are several types of questions an interviewer may ask you

  • General questions (getting to know you)
  • Motivational questions (why you want to work in the role/company)
  • Competency-based questions (past endeavours that will lead to future success)
  • Strength-based questions (what are you good at)
  • Technical questions (questions about your technological expertise)

The Initial Greeting

From as soon as you walk in the building:

  • Walk tall and straight
  • Be grounded before you speak
  • Make eye contact and smile
  • Greet the interviewer(s) - shake hands, fist bump, wave
  • Say who you are clearly
  • Remember to breathe

During the interview

  • What you say:
    • Use examples
    • Be informative – structure your answers
    • Boast modestly
    • Take time over difficult questions - ask for clarification if necessary
  • How you say it:
    • Think about the words you use
    • Talk about I rather than we
    • Structure your main points
      • Top down - start with main point you want to get across and then expand with some detail
      • Number your points - I have three points to make
      • STAR (Situation, Task, Action, Result) - useful for competency-based questions

        For example

        Tell us about a challenge you faced and how you handled it?

        • Situation: In my last job, we were facing a tough deadline and my boss was out for the day.
        • Task:: Our client was expecting a project to be delivered by 5:00 PM, but we were far behind schedule.
        • Action: I took the lead on the project, delegated tasks to the four other team members in a way that I thought would utilise everyone’s strengths best. And then I re-organized my own personal tasks so I could dedicate my entire day to contributing to this project as well.
        • Result: The project was a success and we delivered the work on-time. I went on to lead more projects after that, and used what I learned to be a better project manager.

Send a follow-up message

Send a thank you email to the person/people who interviewed you on the same day. If you do not hear a response, follow up with a second email to explain that you are still interested in the position.

What to include

  • Remind the interview of your conversation (reference specific moment from your conversation)
  • Fix any missteps (forget to mention something obvious/meaningful)
  • Reinforce your candidacy (use info gather during interview)
  • Open the door for future communications

Example of a follow-up message:

Dear Mr. Lee
I sincerely enjoyed meeting with you yesterday and learning more about the content associate position at ACME Media.

Our conversation confirmed my interest in becoming part of ACME Media. I was particularly pleased with the prospect of being able to develop my own article ideas with the head of the bureau, and develop my multimedia skills.

I feel confident that my experiences both in the workplace and in the classroom would enable me to fill the job requirements effectively. Please feel free to contact me if I can provide you with any further information. I look forward eagerly to hearing from you , and thank you again for the courtesy you extended to me.

Sincerely,
Rita Applicant
